Full Stack AI Governance Presented at UCAAT 2025

AI Documentation Platform for EU AI Act Compliance

MERN Stack · TypeScript · AdminJS · RBAC · MongoDB Atlas

When the EU AI Act came into force, it introduced Article 11 — a legal obligation requiring high-risk AI systems to maintain detailed technical documentation across their entire lifecycle. The problem was that no practical, unified tool existed to do this. Organisations were improvising with spreadsheets, scattered PDFs, and ad hoc documentation processes. I built one.

The platform provides a structured, role-aware environment for documenting AI systems from development through deployment and audit. It supports pre-formatted compliance templates, a role-based access control system across three distinct user types — Admin, Contributor, and Auditor — and a scoring mechanism that evaluates each AI system's transparency and trustworthiness based on how comprehensively it's documented.

I validated it against three CE-certified medical AI devices operating in real clinical environments: Vara (breast cancer screening), Annalise (chest imaging), and Us2.ai (cardiac ultrasound) — systems where documentation failures aren't just regulatory problems, they're patient safety failures.

Selected and presented at UCAAT 2025 — the International Workshop on User-Centric Security and Usable Privacy, held in Europe. This was the first practical implementation of an Article 11-compliant documentation tool tested against live, CE-certified medical AI.

Post-Pandemic Readiness: Visualising the World's Fragility

Python · Pandas · Matplotlib · Seaborn · Plotly · Jupyter

After COVID-19, the question that stayed with me wasn't "what happened?" — that had been documented exhaustively. It was: if it happened again tomorrow, which regions would be hit hardest, and why?

This project uses post-pandemic datasets to build a data-driven picture of global health infrastructure readiness. I mapped healthcare capacity, vaccination rollout speed, institutional response lag, and economic resilience across regions — then visualised the gaps in a way that makes the disparities impossible to look away from. Sub-Saharan Africa, parts of South and Southeast Asia, and certain Eastern European countries emerge repeatedly as the most structurally vulnerable — not due to one failure point, but an accumulation of compounding weaknesses.

The goal wasn't to predict doom. It was to ask: what does the data actually say about preparedness, stripped of political framing? The visualisations were designed to be clear enough for a policy conversation, rigorous enough for an academic one.

This work sits at the intersection of data engineering, public health systems, and communication design. It's a reminder that good visualisation isn't decoration — it's argument.

CareForSkin — Skin Condition Detection with Computer Vision

Python · Streamlit · Roboflow · OpenCV · RAG · LLMs

Skincare recommendations online are overwhelming — and almost entirely disconnected from what's actually happening to someone's skin. Most apps ask you to self-diagnose and then match keywords to products. I wanted to build something that looked first, then responded.

CareForSkin takes a user's uploaded selfie, runs it through a Roboflow computer vision model trained to detect specific skin conditions, annotates the detected issues directly on the image, and then generates a personalised routine based on what was actually found — not what the user guessed. The recommendation layer is augmented by a custom RAG-based chatbot trained on a domain-specific skincare knowledge base using LLMs, so users can ask follow-up questions and get contextual, grounded answers rather than generic advice.

The full stack is lightweight and deployable — just Python and Streamlit — which meant I had to make smart decisions about where to use external APIs (Roboflow for the CV model) versus building locally. It's a practical demonstration of how to compose AI capabilities meaningfully rather than stacking them arbitrarily.

Machine Learning Web App

Developer Salary Predictor — From Survey to Streamlit App

Python · scikit-learn · Streamlit · Jupyter · StackOverflow 2023 Dataset

The StackOverflow Developer Survey is one of the richest publicly available datasets on the tech industry — but most analysis of it stops at charts and percentages. I wanted to take it further: build something that lets a developer ask "what would I expect to earn, given my country, experience, and stack?" and get a real, model-backed answer.

The project covers the full ML pipeline: cleaning messy, real-world survey data (nulls, outliers, inconsistent labels), feature engineering for categorical variables like country and education level, then training and comparing three regression models — Decision Tree, Linear Regression, and Random Forest — with formal evaluation using MSE and R-squared. The final model is served through a Streamlit web app with two modes: Predict (input your parameters, get a salary estimate) and Explore (interactive charts showing global salary distributions by region and role).

This project taught me something I keep coming back to: the difference between a notebook and a product is mostly friction reduction. Once the model was good, the challenge became building an interface that made it worth using.

Infrastructure DevSecOps

HashiCorp Vault — Secrets Management for Production Systems

HashiCorp Vault · PKI · Dynamic Secrets · Policy-Based Access Control

Hardcoded credentials in config files. API keys committed to version control. Secrets shared over Slack. These aren't hypothetical risks — they're how most systems actually run until something goes wrong. I wanted to understand how to build the alternative properly, from first principles.

This work covers HashiCorp Vault end-to-end: setting up a secure secrets engine, configuring dynamic secret generation (so credentials are short-lived and per-request rather than static), implementing a PKI infrastructure for internal certificate management, and writing policy rules that enforce least-privilege access across teams. The focus was on understanding the mental model — why Vault is designed the way it is, not just how to run the commands.

For anyone targeting DevOps or platform engineering roles in Germany, especially in regulated sectors, this is table-stakes knowledge. I wanted to be able to say I'd actually done it, not just read about it.

Paired with my broader research into cloud storage and infrastructure design, this work reflects a commitment to understanding security not as a layer bolted on at the end, but as architecture.

Data Engineering Architecture

Data Lakehouses — Why the Architecture Wars Matter

Delta Lake · Apache Iceberg · Databricks · Data Warehouses · Object Storage

For years, data teams had to choose: warehouses for structured, query-able data with strong ACID guarantees, or lakes for cheap, scalable raw storage with flexible schemas. The lakehouse architecture emerged as an attempt to have both — and it's now the backbone of how companies like Databricks, AWS, and Snowflake are positioning their data platforms.

I went deep on this because the decision between warehouse, lake, and lakehouse is one that every cloud engineer will be asked to reason about. The research covers the technical mechanisms that make lakehouses possible — particularly Delta Lake's transaction log and how Apache Iceberg handles schema evolution — as well as the real trade-offs in cost, latency, governance, and operational complexity that are often glossed over in vendor materials.

The result is a clear-eyed analysis of when each architecture makes sense, written for someone who has to actually implement and maintain these systems — not just present them in a slide deck.

Cloud Distributed Systems

IPFS and the Case for Decentralised Cloud Storage

IPFS · Content Addressing · Distributed Architecture · Cloud Comparison

Every file you upload to S3 or Azure Blob is stored at a location — an address that says "this thing lives here." IPFS flips that model: files are addressed by what they are (a hash of their content) rather than where they are. If multiple nodes hold a copy, any of them can serve it. There's no single point of failure, no central actor who can delete or censor a file.

I explored IPFS as part of a broader investigation into what "cloud storage" actually means and what the alternatives look like. The research covers content addressing and its implications for integrity verification, how node discovery and routing work in a peer-to-peer network, and where IPFS genuinely outperforms centralised storage — and where it doesn't. Pinning, gateway latency, and the cold-start problem are real limitations that the promotional material tends to underplay.

The honest conclusion: IPFS isn't a drop-in replacement for S3, but for specific use cases — immutable content delivery, archival storage, systems that need censorship resistance — it's architecturally superior. Understanding both paradigms makes you a better cloud engineer.

I'm drawn to roles that sit at the intersection of engineering and systems-level thinking — teams that ask not just "can we build it?" but "should we, and if so, how do we build it so it doesn't break, exploit, or exclude?" Outside work, I think a lot about how data-driven systems shape public understanding — the pandemic readiness project came out of that. I believe developers have more influence over the world than we're usually comfortable admitting, which means we have more responsibility too.

Regulation as engineering constraint

The EU AI Act, GDPR, and medical device directives aren't obstacles to building — they're specifications. I find that thinking about compliance early produces better systems, not worse ones.

Data that argues

Visualisation is not decoration. When I build charts and maps, the goal is to make the underlying claim undeniable to someone who hasn't spent weeks in the dataset. Good data work changes minds.

Honesty about trade-offs

Every architectural choice has a downside. Every tool has a failure mode. I'd rather be known for saying "here's what this costs" than for overselling what I've built.
